Indian Philosophy & Religion

Exploring India's Spiritual Wisdom Traditions

Sanātana Dharma

Hindu Philosophy

Originating from Vedic traditions (1500 BCE), Hinduism's darśanas (philosophical systems) include:
- Vedānta: Brahman-Ātman non-dualism
- Sāṃkhya: Cosmic dualism of Puruṣa-Prakṛti
- Yoga: Aṣṭāṅga path to kaivalya
The Trimūrti (Brahmā, Viṣṇu, Śiva) represent cosmic functions of creation, preservation, and destruction. Core concepts of karma (action), saṃsāra (rebirth), and mokṣa (liberation) guide ethical living through dharma (duty).

Sacred Texts: Ṛg Veda, Upaniṣads, Bhagavad Gītā

Practices: Pūjā rituals, yajña sacrifices, temple arts

Yoga Darśana

Patañjali's Yoga Sūtras (400 CE) outline aṣṭāṅga yoga:
1. Yama (ethical restraints)
2. Niyama (observances)
3. Āsana (postures)
4. Prāṇāyāma (breath control)
5. Pratyāhāra (sense withdrawal)
6. Dhāraṇā (concentration)
7. Dhyāna (meditation)
8. Samādhi (absorption)
Modern variants like Iyengar and Ashtanga preserve traditional principles while adapting to contemporary needs.

Buddha Dharma

Buddhist Teachings

Founded by Siddhārtha Gautama (563-483 BCE), Buddhism's Four Noble Truths diagnose:
1. Dukkha (suffering)
2. Samudāya (craving origin)
3. Nirodha (cessation)
4. Magga (Eightfold Path)
The Mahāyāna tradition developed bodhisattva ideal and śūnyatā (emptiness) philosophy, preserved in texts like Prajñāpāramitā Sūtras.

Meditative Traditions

Buddhist meditation techniques include:
- Theravāda Vipassanā (insight)
- Zen Zazen (sitting meditation)
- Tibetan Dzogchen (nature of mind)
Monasteries like Nalanda (5th-12th c.) served as early universities preserving these practices through teacher-disciple lineages.

Jain Dharma

Jain Philosophy

Established by Mahāvīra (599-527 BCE), the 24th tīrthaṅkara, Jainism emphasizes:
- Anekāntavāda (multiplicity of viewpoints)
- Syādvāda (conditional predication)
- Ascetic practices for mokṣa
The Ratnatraya (Three Jewels) of right faith, knowledge, and conduct guide followers in observing ahiṃsā (non-violence) through strict vegetarianism and microscopic organism protection.

Ascetic Practices

Jain monastic orders follow 28 mūla guṇas (root vows):
- Sallekhanā (ritual fasting)
- Kayotsarga (meditative posture)
- Pratikramaṇa (repentance ritual)
Lay followers observe 12 vratas (vows) including limitations on professions to minimize harm.

Sikh Dharma

Sikh Philosophy

Founded by Gurū Nānak (1469-1539), Sikhism synthesizes:
- Ik Oṅkār (monotheism)
- Haḍḍī Vāk (divine immanence)
- Mīrī-Pīrī (political-spiritual balance)
The Gurū Granth Sāhib contains 6,000 śabads (hymns) in 31 rāgas, serving as eternal spiritual guide since 1708.

Khalsa Code

The Five K's represent Sikh identity:
1. Kesh (unshorn hair)
2. Kaṅghā (wooden comb)
3. Kaṛā (steel bracelet)
4. Kirpān (ceremonial dagger)
5. Kacchera (cotton undergarment)
Daily practices include nīt nem (prayer cycle) and volunteering in gurdwārā kitchens serving 100,000+ free meals daily worldwide.
